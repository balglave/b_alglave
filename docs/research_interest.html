Research interests

Massive data sources data are progressively becoming available in the field of ecology, agronomy, epidemiology or climatology. Hunting records, healthcare data or fishermen catch declarations are common examples of such kind of data. They can reveal very informative of spatial processes such as fish or animal distribution, distribution of some disease, etc. However they also raise key methodological challenges to (1) properly integrate these data sources and (2) to infer the ecological processes of interest and extract the ecological information from these complex data sources.

My research focus on the development of statistical methods to infer spatio-temporal ecological processes from these massive and heterogeneous data sources. This implies developing methods that accounts for potential non-standardized sampling of the data as well as differences in the spatial resolution of the data sources. Provided these are handled, these data should be used to infer ecological mechanisms such as population dynamics at fine spatio-temporal resolution. Finally, as these data often regroup a large set of diverse variables, this requires to develop multivariate analysis suited for spatio-temporal analysis.

Handling observation and sampling issues

Two major issues lies in the modelling of the sampling and of the observation process of the data.

For instance, sampling of these massive data sources is not necessarily standardized and in some cases it can be preferential towards the process under study (preferential sampling). In such case, not accounting for the sampling process in inference may introduce a bias in model predictions.

The data sources may not have the same spatial resolution. When roughly accounted for, this can lead to biased estimates too. Such issue is commonly referred as change of support.

Developing methods that handle these issues is a major challenge to infer spatial processes from these massive data.

During my PhD, I developed spatio-temporal hierarchical models based on fisheries data sources to map fish distribution. It feeds on massive and spatially rough catch declaration data and standardized fine-scale scientific survey data. It accounts both for preferential sampling of commercial data and for the difference of spatial support between the different data sources.

Spatio-temporal modelling of population dynamics processes.

Population dynamics model are widely used in ecology to estimate the demographic processes affecting of populations. They are extensively used for conservation and management of wildlife species. In fisheries science, these are major tools for estimating the level of exploitation of marine harvested population. These models are most often non-spatial; however, it is now recognized that including spatial dimension in those model is crucial because demographic processes have spatial variability. The development of new tools for inference (INLA, TMB) has enable huge computation gains in inference of spatio-temporal models and to move towards spatio-temporal population dynamics model.
